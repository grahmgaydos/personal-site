Procurement and Process:
what AI governance moved — and what it can't see.

An interrupted time series analysis of civilian federal AI contracting around the 2024–2025 OMB AI memoranda and their repeal. Data from USAspending.gov, October 2021 – September 2025 (extended to January 2026 for the repeal test).

When the 2024 requirements actually took force — the 1 December 2024 deadline — civilian AI contracting accelerated, and the rise was specific to AI. A later deregulatory replacement produced no comparable AI-specific break. And the most aggressive access vehicle, OneGov, barely registers in the contract record at all.

FINDING

The decisive break is enforcement, not announcement. At the 1 December 2024 compliance deadline — when the memos' contract requirements for high-risk AI took force — civilian AI actions accelerated by ~5.9 a month, significant across OLS, Newey-West and GLS, and the rise holds as a share of civilian IT even after absorbing seasonality. The separate release-date breaks (M-24-10, M-24-18) are weaker and method-dependent. After the April 2025 repeal, raw actions fell ~7%/month — but civilian IT fell at the same rate and the AI share of IT did not move, so the decline rode a broad contraction, not a deregulation-specific reversal.

03 — The repeal

Repeal without reversal.

On 3 April 2025 the Biden memoranda were rescinded and replaced (M-25-21/22), shifting the posture from risk-management requirements toward accelerated adoption and streamlined acquisition. Extending the series to January 2026 gives a second break to test — and it comes back a clean negative.

Raw civilian AI actions did fall after the repeal, by about 7% a month. But this was not an AI-specific reversal. Civilian IT contracting overall fell at essentially the same rate (~7%/month), while total civilian contracting fell more slowly (~3%/month) — and the AI share of civilian IT showed no significant change (p = 0.49). AI rode a broad 2025 contraction in civilian contracting rather than responding to the repeal. A re-channelling check rules out the alternative that AI simply migrated onto reseller or OneGov vehicles: reseller-named AI fell alongside directly-named AI rather than rising to absorb it.

This points to an asymmetry. An operational deadline that made AI contractable left a clear, AI-specific trace in the record; a later replacement that relaxed and redirected those rules did not produce a comparable break. The contrast is not a clean test of regulation versus deregulation — the episodes differ in mechanism, timing, and surrounding conditions — but it suggests procurement governance can be adoption-enabling precisely when it turns a risky technology into something agencies can review, justify, and contract for.

04 — The blind spot

The lever you can't see.

The most aggressive recent intervention is the one the contract record is least able to register. GSA's OneGov arrangement (live from spring 2025) negotiates near-free, enterprise-wide access to commercial AI tools — ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini — by treating the government as a single customer. By GSA's own account it has reached on the order of 3.4 million users and avoided roughly $1.15 billion in cost. In the federal contract record analysed here it is effectively invisible: it surfaces only as a handful of nominal-value line items — reseller subscriptions recorded at $1 — and license quantities appear in under 1% of AI rows. It cannot be tested as an interruption at all.

Two further blind spots reinforce the point. Declared-but-text-silent AI: 4,518 contract transactions across 342 awards that agencies declared as AI but that keyword identification misses, only 853 of which carry a usable date. Sub-prime AI: at least $112M — up to ~$164M — of AI work (Palantir, C3.AI, Databricks, Clearview) sits in subawards beneath 121 prime contracts that do not read as AI at all.

The instrument that records what government buys is a poor lens on how AI actually enters government — and that visibility gap is not a footnote to the analysis but part of its argument. The price-and-access lever moves real adoption while staying almost entirely outside the contract record.

05 — Question

What does federal AI procurement guidance actually do?

The OMB memoranda M-24-10 (March 2024) and M-24-18 (September 2024) set out detailed expectations for how civilian federal agencies were to acquire, manage, and govern AI systems. The framing was enabling: clarifying procurement pathways, removing friction, raising the bar on responsible adoption. Their 2025 replacements (M-25-21/22) and the OneGov vehicle then pulled in a different direction.

But what happens in the contract data? Did agencies sign more AI contracts, larger ones, or both? Does administrative guidance produce signalling without substance — language that changes faster than the spending lines underneath it? And does relaxing the rules move procurement the way imposing them did?

This thesis treats the memos and their repeal as quasi-experimental interventions, runs an interrupted time series on the civilian contracting universe, and tests whether the effect appears at the extensive margin (frequency), the intensive margin (per-contract value), or — in OneGov's case — outside the contract record entirely.

06 — Method

Interrupted time series, civilian universe.

Monthly aggregation of civilian AI-related contract actions, identified through a layered filter combining NAICS coding and technical-vocabulary keywords, with supplementary capture for named AI vendors and products and a cross-walk to the OMB AI use-case inventory. Defense and intelligence agencies are excluded throughout — the memoranda govern civilian agencies, and defense contracting is a structurally distinct procurement regime, so it is dropped rather than used as a placebo.

07 — Implication

What this means for how we read AI policy.

The headline pattern — AI contracting accelerating in frequency once the requirements took force, with dollars-per-buy holding closer to trend — suggests the memos lowered the procedural cost of writing an AI contract more than they unlocked larger procurements. Agencies appear to be experimenting at higher cadence, not committing larger budgets to flagship buys.

That has practical implications for anyone reading federal AI activity from the outside. A surge in AI contract counts after a guidance memo is not, in itself, evidence of a budget event. It is at least as plausibly evidence of bureaucratic permission — that agencies already capable of running AI procurements found the process less friction-laden after the memos arrived.

The repeal makes the same point in reverse. Removing or replacing the rules did not produce a symmetric procurement response — governance left a stronger institutional trace than its rollback did. And OneGov shows the limits of reading AI policy from contract data at all: the most aggressive access intervention moves enormous adoption while remaining almost invisible in the record.

It also points to where to look next. Where the action count rises faster than the dollar volume, the interesting questions concern what kinds of AI federal agencies are actually buying — small per-seat licences for off-the-shelf tools rather than large bespoke development contracts. The measurement-visibility analysis develops this directly; the thesis takes it further.
