Procurement and Process:
what the 2024 OMB AI memos moved.

An interrupted time series analysis of civilian federal AI contracting, before and after the 2024 OMB AI procurement memoranda. Headline finding and underlying data drawn from USAspending.gov, October 2020 – September 2025.

After the OMB memos, civilian agencies signed AI contracts far more often — roughly half again as many per month — but the dollars per contract fell. Activity expanded; ambition held steady.

FINDING

The two series tell different stories. After M-24-10, the action count shifted up sharply and stayed there — agencies are now writing AI contracts at roughly +50% the prior monthly cadence. The dollar series looks much closer to its pre-memo trend, with the familiar September fiscal-year-end spikes still doing most of the work. The OMB memos appear to have changed how often agencies procure AI, more than how much they spend each time they do. Significance testing, a Defense placebo, and falsification design are reported in the thesis itself.

03 — Question

What does federal AI procurement guidance actually do?

The OMB memoranda M-24-10 (March 2024) and M-24-18 (September 2024) set out detailed expectations for how civilian federal agencies were to acquire, manage, and govern AI systems. The framing was enabling: clarifying procurement pathways, removing friction, raising the bar on responsible adoption.

But what happens in the contract data? Did agencies sign more AI contracts, larger ones, or both? Or does administrative guidance produce signalling without substance — language that changes faster than the spending lines underneath it?

This thesis treats the memos as a quasi-experimental intervention, runs an interrupted time series on the civilian contracting universe, and tests whether the effect appears at the extensive margin (frequency), the intensive margin (per-contract value), or both.

04 — Method

Interrupted time series, civilian universe.

Monthly aggregation of all civilian AI-related contract actions, identified through a layered filter that combines NAICS coding, technical-vocabulary keywords, and supplementary capture for direct AI-lab recipients (Scale AI, Databricks Federal), named frontier products (ChatGPT, Microsoft Copilot, Azure OpenAI), and cloud-mediated AI services (IBM Watson, Google Dialogflow, AWS Polly, Vertex AI).

05 — Implication

What this means for how we read AI policy.

The headline pattern — many more AI contracts, only modestly more AI dollars — is more revealing than a uniform surge would be. It suggests the memos lowered the procedural cost of writing an AI contract more than they unlocked larger procurements. Agencies appear to be experimenting at higher cadence, not committing larger budgets to flagship buys.

That has practical implications for anyone reading federal AI activity from the outside. A surge in AI contract counts after a guidance memo is not, in itself, evidence of a budget event. It is at least as plausibly evidence of bureaucratic permission — that agencies who were already capable of running AI procurements found the process less friction-laden after the memos arrived.

It also points to where to look next. If the action count is rising faster than the dollar volume, the interesting questions concern what kinds of AI federal agencies are actually buying — small per-seat licences for off-the-shelf tools (ChatGPT Enterprise, Microsoft Copilot, Google Dialogflow), rather than large bespoke development contracts. The supplementary-capture analysis above hints at this directly. The thesis develops it more carefully.
