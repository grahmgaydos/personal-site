§ 09 · The Filter

§ 09 · The Filter
Tier 1
High-confidence AI signals
Contracts whose own description contains an unambiguous AI keyword. The model and the task are explicit.
"machine learning" "natural language processing" "computer vision" "large language model" "generative AI"
These are the contracts nobody disputes. The narrow base of the funnel.
§ 09 · The Filter
Tier 2
Adjacent capabilities & product names
Brand-name AI products, named platforms, and tightly AI-adjacent capabilities that don't always say "AI" but are AI.
Palantir Foundry · AIP Anthropic · OpenAI · xAI "predictive analytics" "chatbot" / "virtual assistant" "document intelligence"
Calibrated against the OMB inventory — vendors agencies declared as AI.
§ 09 · The Filter
Tier 3
Exclusions: M-24-18 carve-outs & false-positive screens
A negative filter, not a positive one. Tier 3 removes what shouldn't count — the carve-outs the OMB memos themselves call out, plus known contamination patterns.
commodity-embedded AI (M-24-18 carve-out) pure R&D contracts (M-24-18 carve-out) bare-acronym noise (part numbers, etc.) modifications to non-AI parents
Applied only to description-route hits — vendor matches and OMB-inventory joins are untouched.

§ 09 · The Filter · The Substring Problem

The Boeing 737 has thousands of AIrline parts.
None of them are AI.

Raw substring matches catch every "A-I" inside other words — airline, airframe, Airbus, Cairo, maintain. Without token-boundary parsing, half the haystack would be parts catalogues.
Fix: word-boundary regex + tier-aware exclusions + manual spot-checks on the top vendors

§ 10 · Robustness (briefly)
Placebo dates
No detectable breaks at random dates inside the same window — the effect is not an artefact of any-date-anywhere.
Alternative keyword sets
Narrower and broader keyword cuts yield the same direction and similar magnitudes.
Newey-West standard errors
Autocorrelation-robust SEs hold the significance of the Dec 1 break and the repeal asymmetry.
As share of federal IT
Denominator-adjusted series confirm enforcement effect; the repeal becomes statistically null once IT trend is accounted for.

§ 11 · Test 1 · M-24-10 · Release-date break

Did the release of the scaffolding memo move contracting?

M-24-10 · Mar 28 2024
Governance-rules memo · release-date break
+6.5 / mo
slope shift · p = 0.008 (NW) · null under OLS & GLS · suggestive (1/3)
Level shift null across all methods. Slope reaches significance only under Newey-West. Suggestive, not robust — reported with the caveat that the short, seasonal series leaves the per-memo estimate inference-method dependent.
§ 11 · Test 2 · M-24-18 · Release-date break

Did the release of the acquisition memo move contracting?

M-24-18 · Sep 24 2024
Acquisition-mandate memo · entangled with FY-end
−60.3 / mo
level shift · p < 0.001 across methods · drops to −30.5 under month-FE
Memo dated five days before fiscal year-end. The level coefficient is collinear with the September spike. Half disappears once month fixed effects absorb the FY boundary — read with the seasonality caveat.
§ 11 · Headline · Dec 1 2024 · Implementation break

Did the operationalisation of the rules move contracting?

Dec 1 2024 · Implementation deadline
Shared compliance date for both memoranda
+5.91 / mo
slope shift · Newey-West p < 0.001 · robust (3/3 methods)
The headline result. Robust under OLS, Newey-West, and GLS-AR(1). Survives month fixed effects and the AI-share-of-IT normalisation. Procurement behaviour tracked the operationalisation, not the announcement.
§ 11 · Finding 1

The movement is at operationalisation, not announcement.

§ 12 · Finding 2 · Regulation vs. Deregulation

Counter­intuitively: building the regime up moved behaviour. Tearing it down didn't.

§ 12 · The expectation

After the April 2025 repeal, we'd expect AI procurement to trend up — rules lifted, fewer hoops.

post-repeal slope · raw counts
−6.3 / mo
civilian AI contract actions · Apr 2025 onwards · Newey-West p = 0.001
The opposite happened. Raw AI contracting fell. So far, looks like deregulation moved the lever — just in the other direction.
post-repeal slope · as share of civilian IT
−6.3
≈ 0
AI-share-of-IT · Newey-West p = 0.31 · null
As a share of civilian IT contracting, the post-repeal change is essentially zero. AI fell at the same rate as civilian IT overall (−7.0% vs −7.1%/mo). It rode the contraction; it didn't cause one.
§ 12 · The asymmetry

Procurement behaviour tracked the operationalisation of requirements far more than their removal.

Prime-contract data sees the tip. Subaward routing hides AI inside otherwise non-AI contracts — at least $112M identifiable in subaward filings.
§ 15 · Three holes in the same instrument

Each problem matters. Each is visible for a different reason.

01
Declared but missing
Half of OMB-declared AI projects don't surface in primes — vehicles, IDIQs, and BPA call orders that read as "IT services" rather than "AI."
02
OneGov compression
A $1.15B program priced at $1/agency/year. Contract action counts capture it. Dollar measures do not.
03
Subaward routing
$112M+ in identifiable AI buried inside otherwise non-AI prime contracts. Visible in subaward filings, invisible in prime-level outcome data.
§ 15 · The Takeaway

No single instrument sees the whole thing. Measurement-visibility is the contribution.

Four Claims
  1. Enforcement — not announcement — is what moves civilian AI contracting. The Dec 1 cliff is the active ingredient, not the rule-text.
  2. Regulation and deregulation are asymmetric: rules land hard, repeals land soft. The reversal does not look like a mirror of the imposition.
  3. Governance arrives through the writing of contracts, not the funding of them: detectable in action counts, invisible in obligated dollars.
  4. Measurement-visibility is the strongest original contribution: no single instrument sees the whole picture — declared, OneGov, subawards each show different slices.
Caveats kept visible

Civilian-only cohort (no DoD, no IC). Prime-contract data only — subawards and IDIQ call orders are partial. DOGE-era contraction is a partial confound on the deregulation null. Substring carve-outs and dedup do real work but are not perfect.
